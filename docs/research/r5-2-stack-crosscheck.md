# RESEARCH 5.2 — Timeline UI Build-vs-Buy & Realtime Collaboration Stack Selection

## TL;DR
- **Timeline (Part 1):** For a solo/small team on Vite + React 18 that needs deep bespoke theming, clean print/PDF, and genuinely accessible drag, **build a custom virtualised DOM-over-CSS-grid timeline using dnd-kit (or react-aria) for accessible drag** — 8 lanes and low-thousands of bookings are well within DOM-virtualisation limits, and no library restyles cleanly to a bespoke dark-graphite/gold system without pain. If you must buy to ship faster, **FullCalendar Premium resource-timeline ($480/dev, one-time) is the best-value commercial option** because its license explicitly permits SaaS, it's the lightest paid lib (~100 KB gzip), it renders DOM (print-friendly), and it exposes `--fc-*` CSS variables. **Bryntum Scheduler Pro** is the most capable but heaviest (multi-MB), weakest on print, and requires a per-quote OEM license for SaaS. **Syncfusion** is free under the Community License while you're under $1M revenue / <10 employees / <5 devs / <$3M outside capital, but that eligibility evaporates as you scale. **vis-timeline** is free (MIT/Apache-2.0) but bundles Moment.js and is effectively maintenance-only.
- **Realtime (Part 2):** **Build server-authoritative WebSockets directly on Fastify + @fastify/websocket**, with an in-memory presence + per-block lock registry, last-write-wins persistence to Neon, and no `LISTEN/NOTIFY`. A booking calendar is a locking/LWW domain, **not** a text-CRDT domain, so Yjs/Liveblocks CRDT machinery is overkill. This honors the "avoid heavy dependencies" bias and fits <20 concurrent users on one Railway instance easily.
- **The one hard constraint to design around now:** Railway has **no sticky sessions** and can terminate long connections, so build reconnect/resync and keepalive from day one and keep all shared state in a single process (or externalise to Redis) before you ever run a second replica.

## Details

### PART 1 — Build vs. buy for the multi-lane timeline

**1) Licensing.**

- **Bryntum Scheduler Pro** uses two license types. The **End-User License (EUL)** is perpetual, per-named-developer, and applies only to apps "where end-users do not pay for access, such as a company intranet application." On published EUL pricing, Bryntum's CEO stated on the record that "Our Small Team license starts at USD 2820 (3 developers)" (~$940/dev; the store page lists per-dev figures around $1,050–$1,100/dev for larger tiers, so treat $2,820/3-dev as the authoritative floor). A **single-developer EUL** is offered only to businesses "with less than 10 employees and USD 1M annual revenue." Critically, **a commercial SaaS product requires the OEM license**, which is subscription-based and quote-only ("To use our UI components in commercial products and SaaS type applications … you require an OEM license"). So Bryntum is cheap-ish to prototype internally but has an unknown, negotiated cost the moment you go multi-tenant SaaS.

- **Syncfusion** offers a **Community License at no cost** to organizations with "less than $1 million USD in annual gross revenue, 5 or fewer developers, and 10 or fewer total employees." Per the Essential Studio EULA §4.2.k.3.2, an entity "may not have ever received more than Three Million United States Dollars ($3,000,000.00 USD) in capital from an outside source such as private equity or venture capital." Syncfusion staff have confirmed on their own forums that a single-developer, sub-$1M, multi-tenant cloud-hosted SaaS using the Scheduler is permitted, and that "the redistribution is completely covered. It's only your revenue that matters." **Two catches:** (a) eligibility ends the moment you cross any threshold, forcing a paid Essential Studio purchase; and (b) EULA §4.2.k.11 states "Community Licenses never include access to source code editions," and there's a no-white-label/no-standalone-redistribution clause. Free now, scaling liability later — and you pull in a 1,600-component suite for one widget.

- **FullCalendar** premium plugins (including `resource-timeline` and Print Optimization) are tri-licensed. The commercial license "starting at $480" **explicitly permits commercial/SaaS use** and source-code editing (but not redistribution of modifications). Third-party marketplace data (Vendr) reports the tier structure as "one developer can access the premium features for $480, while licenses for multiple developers offer discounts: $720 for two developers, $1560 for five, and $2400 for ten." The license is valid for one year and includes upgrades, full source-code access, and email support; renewals are "50% discount" if before expiry, "25% discount" after, and you may perpetually use the last version released before expiry. For a SaaS where you don't hand clients editable source, "you only need a single FullCalendar Premium license in your company's name." This is the cleanest SaaS story of the paid options.

- **vis-timeline** is dual-licensed **Apache-2.0 OR MIT** — free for commercial/SaaS with only attribution.

- **Custom build** uses **dnd-kit (MIT)** and/or **react-aria (Apache-2.0)** — both free for SaaS.

**2) Bundle size.** FullCalendar core+resource-timeline is roughly **~100 KB gzipped** and v6 self-injects its CSS and tree-shakes reasonably. **vis-timeline** ships Moment.js (its own maintainers flag Moment as ~10% of a ~1.4 MB raw bundle) plus vis-data and hammerjs. **Bryntum is by far the heaviest**: its own support-forum threads describe adding **~1 MB gzipped** to a real app, "~5 MB" production JS, and that the module bundles "do contain all classes … but in the opposite they are quite big" with no simple tree-shaking (a "thin bundle" effort was still in progress at the time of those threads). **Syncfusion** is modular (per-component ej2 packages and per-component CSS), so lighter than Bryntum but still pulls several `ej2-*` dependencies (buttons, calendars, dropdowns, inputs, navigations, popups) for the Schedule. A **custom build** is smallest — dnd-kit's core is tens of KB and you ship only the code you write.

**3) Theming depth.** This axis strongly favors DOM-rendered, CSS-variable systems for a bespoke "quiet luxury" dark-graphite/gold aesthetic:
- **FullCalendar** exposes `--fc-*` CSS custom properties that in v6 can be overridden "anywhere you'd like" with no build-system config, and it renders true DOM nodes (via Preact/React), so every element is inspectable and overridable. Note: v7 is introducing a new theme API and the DOM structure is "unstable across major versions," so **pin to v6 and write CSS against v6**.
- **Syncfusion** has CSS-variable themes, an online **Theme Studio**, and Figma design-token export — capable but you're theming a large opinionated system.
- **Bryntum** v7 introduced CSS-variable theming with light/dark per theme, but real-world usage still requires overriding `.b-*` internals and occasional `!important` (as shown in their own forum styling examples).
- **vis-timeline** renders plain DOM styleable by CSS class but has no CSS-variable system and a dated default look.
- **Custom** wins outright: it's your DOM and your tokens, no override wars.

**4) Print/PDF fidelity.** Venue day-sheets that print cleanly favor DOM renderers.
- **FullCalendar** ships a dedicated **Print Optimization** premium plugin and, being DOM-based, honors print stylesheets well.
- **Syncfusion** Schedule/Gantt have built-in print and PDF/Excel export.
- **Bryntum** is weakest here: it officially recommends **PDF export via a separate export server** rather than browser print ("We don't support Printing I'm afraid, we recommend you to use PDF export"), and has documented export bugs — custom CSS classes dropped on export, last row cut off, and a changelog entry noting "Scheduler print feature breaks when printing multiple pages." A server-export pipeline is more moving parts for a simple day-sheet.
- **vis-timeline / custom** rely on DOM print; custom gives full print-CSS control.

**5) Drag accessibility.** None of the commercial timeline libraries offer best-in-class keyboard/screen-reader drag for moving/resizing event blocks; they are mouse-first with partial ARIA. This is the strongest argument for a custom build:
- **dnd-kit** ships a **KeyboardSensor** (enabled by default) adhering to ARIA authoring practices: draggables get `tabIndex=0`, `role`, `aria-roledescription`, `aria-describedby`; Space/Enter to pick up, arrow keys to move, Enter to drop, Escape to cancel; plus a customizable **live-region announcement** system (`onDragStart`/`onDragOver`/`onDragEnd`). For a timeline you'd write a custom `coordinateGetter` mapping arrow keys to time-slot increments (e.g., snap to 15/30-min steps) and lane changes.
- **react-aria (Adobe)** offers accessible DnD implemented "from scratch" for keyboard and screen reader: Enter starts a page-wide drag mode, **Tab cycles only between valid drop targets**, Enter drops, Escape cancels; localized screen-reader announcements in 30+ languages; touch screen-reader support. Its `useDraggableCollection`/`useDroppableCollection` model maps well to a lane/row grid, and drop indicators must use correct roles. React-aria is the more rigorous, more opinionated choice; dnd-kit is lighter and more flexible for a canvas/DOM hybrid.

**Custom build assessment (8 lanes, low-thousands of bookings, long horizons).** At this scale a **DOM-with-windowing** approach is sufficient and simplest:
- **Grid/background layer:** render time gridlines and lane backgrounds as CSS (or a single canvas/SVG layer if you want zoomable tick density without thousands of DOM nodes). Static per zoom level and cheap.
- **Interactive blocks layer:** render only booking blocks in the visible time window × visible lanes as absolutely-positioned DOM elements (windowing on both axes). With 8 lanes and only the visible horizon mounted, you render tens-to-low-hundreds of nodes at once even if the dataset is thousands — DOM handles this comfortably. Prior art proves the pattern: **Planby** virtualises both axes ("Loading 10,000 events looks identical to loading 100 … events outside the viewport are never mounted in the DOM"), **SVAR React Gantt** (MIT) demos 10,000 tasks, **gantt-schedule-timeline-calendar (GSTC)** handles "hundreds of thousands of items" via virtual scroll, and **react-timeline-gantt** uses virtual rendering for "100 thousands records." Study these for windowing math and interaction models; you likely don't need canvas for the blocks at all at 8 lanes.
- **Effort estimate:** a polished custom timeline with drag-move, drag-resize, keyboard accessibility, snapping, overlap handling, and print CSS is a meaningful but bounded build — on the order of several engineer-weeks for a competent React dev, versus days to integrate a commercial lib. The trade is upfront time now vs. license cost, theming freedom, and accessibility control later. (Directional, not a quote — validate with a spike.)

**Verdict Part 1.** Given the explicit bias against heavy deps, the bespoke dark-graphite/gold design system, clean-print requirement, and keyboard-drag requirement — **the custom virtualised DOM timeline with dnd-kit (or react-aria) is the best long-term fit and the only option that fully satisfies all five axes.** The pragmatic fallback to ship in weeks not months is **FullCalendar Premium resource-timeline** ($480/dev one-time, SaaS-legal, ~100 KB, DOM print, `--fc-*` theming), accepting weaker keyboard-drag. Avoid Bryntum unless you need its constraint-based scheduling engine and can absorb the OEM cost and bundle/print weaknesses. Treat Syncfusion Community as a short-term freebie only if you're certain to stay under the thresholds. vis-timeline only if budget is truly zero and you accept Moment.js and a dated look.

### PART 2 — Realtime layer

**Why a booking calendar is NOT a CRDT domain.** CRDTs (Yjs) exist to merge concurrent edits to the *same document* (especially text) without conflicts and without a central authority. A multi-room booking calendar is the opposite: you *want* a single source of truth, and concurrent edits to the same booking block should be prevented by a **lock**, not silently merged. Even the Yjs ecosystem says so: "If you only need occasional 'Save' + locking, a simpler last-write-wins approach might be enough." Naive last-write-wins can produce logical errors, so the right model is **server-authoritative writes with per-block locking + optimistic UI**. This kills the case for Yjs and for the CRDT-storage features of Liveblocks.

**Railway platform constraints (design around these).**
- **No sticky sessions.** Railway staff, on the record on Central Station: "We do not currently support sticky sessions, and there is no timeline to share on when it might be available… the recommended approach is to externalize your task state (e.g., into Redis or a database) so any replica can serve any client's reconnect, rather than relying on load-balancer affinity." **Implication: a single instance is fine; the moment you add a replica, in-memory presence/locks break unless externalised.**
- **Connection duration — conflicting docs, so build defensively.** Railway's Socket.IO guide states a **15-minute maximum request duration** ("WebSocket connections open longer than 15 minutes are terminated"), while Railway's SSE-vs-WebSockets guide states WebSockets "are exempt from request timeouts and can stay open indefinitely, even while idle." These two docs conflict; community reports also cite ~10-minute idle disconnects. The safe posture regardless: **application-level keepalive pings every 10–30s** plus client reconnect with backoff and state resync.
- **Cost of long-lived connections.** Railway is usage-priced (Memory $0.00000386/GB-sec ≈ ~$10/GB-month; CPU $0.00000772/vCPU-sec ≈ ~$20/vCPU-month; egress $0.05/GB). A 24/7 service at a constant 1 vCPU + 1 GB RAM bills ≈ $30/month in compute; Hobby is $5/mo (includes $5 usage), Pro $20/mo (includes $20 usage per seat). <20 concurrent WebSockets is trivial load — you pay for an always-on small container, not connection volume.

**Neon connection model for a long-lived Fastify server.** Key nuance: the `@neondatabase/serverless` driver's HTTP/WebSocket modes are designed for *serverless/edge* where you can't hold a TCP pool. **Your Fastify process on Railway is a long-lived server, not serverless** — so open a normal pooled TCP connection pool once at module scope and reuse it (Neon's own guidance: "Long-running or shared-runtime environments → node-postgres (pg). … Open a pg pool once at module scope and reuse it across requests"). Use Neon's **pooled endpoint (`-pooler`, PgBouncer)** for normal query traffic.

Crucially for realtime fan-out: **`LISTEN/NOTIFY` does NOT work through Neon's pooled (PgBouncer transaction-mode) endpoint** — "PgBouncer in transaction mode returns the connection to the pool after each transaction, so session-scoped features like SET, LISTEN/NOTIFY … don't work." If you wanted DB-driven fan-out you'd need a **dedicated direct (unpooled) connection** held open for `LISTEN`. **But at single-instance scale you don't need LISTEN/NOTIFY at all** — the Fastify process is itself the fan-out hub: it applies the write to Neon and broadcasts to its own connected clients in-memory. Keep `LISTEN/NOTIFY` (on a direct connection) in reserve only for the future multi-instance case as a lightweight cross-instance bus.

**The Fastify WebSocket build.** `@fastify/websocket` is built on `ws@8` and exposes the raw `ws` socket to your handler. It has **no built-in rooms or pub/sub** — you broadcast by iterating either `fastify.websocketServer.clients` or (better) a manually maintained connection registry, checking `client.readyState === 1` before sending (Nearform's documented pattern: iterate `fastify.websocketServer.clients`, filter out the sender, `client.send(message)`). Concretely:
- **Connection registry:** a `Map<userId, socket>` (or `Set` of connection objects carrying `{userId, name, color, socket, viewingRange}`), maintained on connect/disconnect.
- **Presence:** on connect/disconnect and on view change, broadcast a compact presence payload (who's viewing, cursor/lane hover, avatar color) to all clients. Ephemeral — never persist it.
- **Per-block edit locking:** an in-memory `Map<bookingId, {lockedBy, lockedAt}>`. On "start editing," client requests a lock; server grants if free (authoritative), broadcasts `lock` to others (who render the block greyed/locked), and sets a TTL so a dropped editor's lock auto-expires (belt-and-braces with the disconnect handler releasing locks). On save, server writes to Neon (LWW), releases the lock, and broadcasts the new state.
- **Reconnect/offline:** client uses exponential backoff; on reconnect it re-authenticates, re-subscribes, and pulls a fresh snapshot (or a "changes since timestamp" delta) to resync. Send app-level pings every ~20s to dodge Railway idle/timeout kills.
- **Persistence:** all mutations go through the server to Neon via the pooled `pg`/Drizzle pool; the WebSocket layer is a fan-out/coordination layer atop an authoritative DB.

**Why not the SaaS options here.**
- **Liveblocks** is excellent DX but is external infrastructure that bypasses your Fastify/Neon stack, prices on **MAU** (Free ≤50 MAU; paid tiers upward), and targets CRDT/collab-doc use cases you don't have. Overkill and a paid dependency.
- **PartyKit** now runs on **Cloudflare Durable Objects** (Cloudflare acquired PartyKit in April 2024); a fine realtime primitive but it ties your realtime layer to Cloudflare Workers, not Railway, splitting your backend. Contradicts the "keep it on our stack" bias.
- **Supabase Realtime** is a strong managed option (Broadcast/Presence/Postgres Changes on Elixir/Phoenix; free tier ~200 peak connections / 2M messages) — but it means adopting Supabase's Postgres/infra alongside Neon, or running Realtime against a DB you don't control. Introduces a second platform.
- **Yjs/CRDT** — wrong domain (see above); adds a heavy dependency to solve concurrent-text-merge, a problem you don't have.

## Recommended architecture (Part 2)

**Primary recommendation: server-authoritative WebSockets on Fastify 5 + @fastify/websocket, single Railway instance, in-memory presence + lock registry, authoritative LWW writes to Neon via a module-scope pooled `pg`/Drizzle pool, and no LISTEN/NOTIFY.**

Justification: it's the only option that adds **zero new heavy third-party dependencies**, keeps all state and logic inside the stack you've already committed to (Fastify/Neon/Railway), and maps exactly onto the domain (authoritative locking + LWW, not CRDT merge). At <20 concurrent staff and 8 lanes the load is trivial for a single small Railway container. Presence and per-block locking are a few dozen lines each on top of a connection registry; the hard parts (reconnect/resync, keepalive) are forced on you by Railway regardless of which option you pick, so you may as well own them.

**Explicit fallbacks / escalation path:**
1. **Scaling beyond one Railway instance (or needing zero-downtime deploys):** because Railway has no sticky sessions, add a **Redis pub/sub backplane** on Railway. Each Fastify instance subscribes to Redis; presence/locks move into Redis (with TTLs) so any instance can serve any reconnecting client. This is Railway's own recommended pattern. Alternatively, keep realtime on a **single dedicated WebSocket service** (vertical scale) and horizontally scale only the stateless HTTP API — viable well past this venue's needs.
2. **Multi-tenant fan-out later:** shard rooms by tenant/venue (one logical room per venue); with Redis, route messages by room key. Optionally use Neon **`LISTEN/NOTIFY` on a dedicated direct (unpooled) connection** as a lightweight DB-change bus if writes from other services must propagate to WS clients.
3. **If in-house realtime becomes a maintenance burden as tenants grow:** re-evaluate **Supabase Realtime** (closest managed fit, Postgres-native, presence/broadcast built-in) before Liveblocks/PartyKit, since it's least conceptually distant from your model. Only adopt a CRDT stack if the product pivots toward genuinely concurrent free-form editing (e.g., collaborative notes on a booking), not for the calendar grid itself.

**Benchmarks that should change the recommendation:** move to Redis-backed multi-instance when you approach a single container's comfortable ceiling (hundreds–low-thousands of concurrent sockets, or when you need rolling deploys without dropping everyone); consider a managed realtime SaaS if you exceed multiple thousands of concurrent connections per tenant or need global multi-region presence.

## Recommendations

1. **Now (single venue, ship it):** Build the timeline as a **custom virtualised DOM component** (CSS/canvas grid layer + windowed DOM blocks) with **dnd-kit** for accessible drag (KeyboardSensor + live-region announcements + custom time-snap `coordinateGetter`). Build realtime as **server-authoritative Fastify WebSockets** with in-memory presence + lock registry and LWW writes to Neon via a pooled `pg`/Drizzle pool. Implement **keepalive pings (~20s) + client reconnect/resync** from day one.
2. **If timeline must ship in weeks, not months:** integrate **FullCalendar Premium resource-timeline** ($480/dev, one-time, SaaS-legal), theme via `--fc-*` variables (pin to v6), use its Print Optimization plugin, and layer a keyboard-accessible edit path (modal/keyboard nudges) on top since native keyboard-drag is weak. Revisit a custom build when accessibility/theming friction outweighs the saved time.
3. **Do NOT** adopt Bryntum unless you specifically need its constraint-based scheduling engine — and if you do, budget for the quote-only **OEM license** (SaaS) and its bundle/print weaknesses. **Do NOT** lean on Syncfusion Community as a permanent plan; it's a scaling trap once you cross $1M revenue / 10 employees / 5 devs / $3M outside capital, and it never includes source-code editions.
4. **Externalise realtime state to Redis before adding a second Railway replica** — never rely on sticky sessions (Railway doesn't offer them). Keep `LISTEN/NOTIFY` as a future multi-instance bus on a dedicated direct Neon connection, not through the pooled endpoint.
5. **Keep presence ephemeral and locks TTL'd.** Release locks on disconnect and on TTL expiry to prevent a dropped editor from freezing a booking.

## Caveats
- **FullCalendar tier pricing above $480 (1 dev)** is sourced from the Vendr marketplace ($720/2, $1,560/5, $2,400/10 devs), not the official site (whose tier table renders via client-side JS). Only $480/1-dev is officially confirmed — verify multi-dev pricing on the live purchase page before budgeting.
- **Bryntum OEM/SaaS pricing is quote-only.** The confirmed figure ("Small Team starts at USD 2820 for 3 developers," per Bryntum's CEO) is the internal-use EUL, not the SaaS cost. Get a written OEM quote before assuming affordability.
- **Bundle-size figures** for Bryntum/vis-timeline come largely from vendor forum threads and issue trackers rather than a controlled Vite benchmark; run your own `vite build` + analyzer against a spike for exact numbers in your config.
- **Railway connection-duration behavior is contradicted across Railway's own docs** (15-min cap in the Socket.IO guide vs. "indefinite, even idle" in the SSE-vs-WebSockets guide). Design for reconnect/keepalive regardless, and re-verify against current Railway docs before launch. No-sticky-sessions and usage pricing are current as sourced but can change.
- **Neon was acquired by Databricks (May 2025)**; the serverless-driver and PgBouncer pooling behaviors described are current but worth re-checking. `LISTEN/NOTIFY` not working through the pooled endpoint is a PgBouncer-transaction-mode limitation, not a Neon bug.
- **Syncfusion Community License** terms (no white-label/standalone redistribution; "reserves all rights … to determine eligibility"; no source-code editions) should be read in the current agreement, not from forum paraphrases, if you plan to depend on it.
- Custom-build effort estimates are directional, not a quote; validate with a time-boxed spike (grid + windowing + one drag interaction) before committing.
