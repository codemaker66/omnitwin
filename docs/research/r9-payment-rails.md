# Research Brief R9 — UK Client-Payment Rails for Venviewer (Venue Booking & Event Management SaaS)

## TL;DR
- **Default rails by milestone:** offer **credit card (Stripe) as an option on the booking deposit** (so clients get the Section 75 protection they actively want), but route **large instalments and final balances (£5k–£15k) to open-banking pay-by-bank** (GoCardless Instant Bank Pay/"Pay by Bank" or TrueLayer) — on a £12,000 balance the fee is roughly **£4 by pay-by-bank versus ~£180 domestic (or ~£390 international) by card**, and because the UK bans consumer-card surcharging, the venue eats that difference.
- **Platform structure:** launch on **Stripe Connect with the venue as merchant of record (Standard connected accounts, or destination charges using `on_behalf_of`), with negative-balance liability assigned to Stripe**, paired with **GoCardless for Partners / Embed** for bank rails where GoCardless holds the direct merchant relationship. This keeps Venviewer a pure facilitator, out of the flow of funds — avoiding FCA payment-institution authorisation/safeguarding and containing the catastrophic risk of a venue going insolvent with a large book of future wedding deposits.
- **The core economic fact:** UK law bans surcharging consumer cards, so on card payments the venue absorbs 1.5%+ (domestic) to 3.25%+ (international); pay-by-bank and Direct Debit are 10–40× cheaper on large tickets. The product should present payment-method choice per milestone and legally nudge bank transfer with a discount, while surfacing the refund-mechanics differences in the client portal.

## Key Findings

### 1. The three rails, compared

**Stripe (cards).** UK standard pricing is **1.5% + 20p for UK consumer/standard cards, 2.5% + 20p for EEA cards, and 3.25% + 20p for international cards**, with an additional up-to-2% currency-conversion fee where applicable. There are no setup or monthly fees. The chargeback/dispute fee is £20 (charged win or lose). Card failure/decline rates on card-not-present run materially higher than bank debit. Payout timing to a UK merchant is T+3 settlement on a rolling schedule; the **first payout is typically held ~7 days** after the first successful charge. Critically for a venue: **Stripe does not return the original processing fee on a refund** (Stripe's own support docs: "processing fees from the original transaction aren't returned") — on a refunded £12,000 balance the venue is out ~£180 in non-recoverable fees, and refunds take 5–10 business days to reach the customer.

**GoCardless / Bacs Direct Debit.** Standard plan is **1% + 20p capped at £4 per transaction**, but with a **high-value transaction (HVT) surcharge of 0.3% on the portion of a domestic Bacs payment above £2,000** (uncapped). GoCardless confirms: "For UK Bank debit transactions of £2,000 or more, we charge an additional 0.3% fee on the portion of transaction above the £2,000 threshold... there are no high-value transaction fees for Instant Bank Pay payments." So the £4 cap is misleading at wedding-balance sizes: a £12,000 Direct Debit costs £4 + 0.3%×£10,000 = **£34**. First payment from a new mandate takes ~7 working days; mandate setup adds friction. Recurring Bacs settles T+2/T+3. Failure modes: insufficient funds, mandate cancellation. The **Direct Debit Guarantee** is the key risk: it is **unlimited in time and amount**, entitling the payer to an immediate, full refund via an indemnity claim, clawed back from the merchant ~14 working days later — GoCardless explicitly states it cannot dispute indemnity claims on the merchant's behalf, and it recommends high-value one-off payments use a push method (bank transfer) rather than Direct Debit.

**Open-banking pay-by-bank (GoCardless Instant Bank Pay / TrueLayer + others).** Bank-to-bank over Faster Payments, instant/near-instant settlement (funds same day if authorised before 11am with GoCardless IBP). **GoCardless Instant Bank Pay (now branded "Pay by Bank") is priced at the merchant's plan rate — Standard 1% + 20p capped at £4 — and crucially has NO high-value surcharge**, so a £5,000 or £12,000 IBP payment costs just £4. TrueLayer is the UK market leader — it states it "process[es] almost half of all UK Pay by Bank transactions," and in 2024 written evidence to Parliament's Financial Services Regulation Committee stated it "process[es] one third of all UK open banking payments, including over 1 million variable recurring payments (VRPs) per month" — but its **pricing is quote-only/sales-gated**. Third-party benchmarks put UK open-banking payment initiation at roughly **0.1%–1.0% or ~20p–50p flat per transaction** (Noda publicly advertises "from 0.1%"; Volt's implied ~2.1% take rate is a high-end outlier; TrueLayer, Yapily, Token.io, Nuapay and Plaid UK are all confirmed quote-only). There is no chargeback scheme and **no native refund rail** — providers bolt on refunds (TrueLayer's closed-loop refund/Payouts API is instant but only back to the original payer, capped at the original amount; GoCardless processes pay-by-bank refunds only on request and only where the refund feature/Pro plan is enabled, and IBP with direct settlement cannot be refunded through GoCardless at all). Consumer protection is weaker: **no Section 75, no chargeback**, but payers retain Payment Services Regulations 2017 rights (unauthorised-transaction refunds), Consumer Rights Act 2015 rights, and APP-fraud reimbursement (mandatory from 7 October 2024).

UK adoption is now mainstream: per Open Banking Limited, "nearly 33 million Pay by Bank transactions [were] processed in November 2025 alone," with more than 16 million active users, and **Amazon UK launched Pay by Bank with TrueLayer on 10 February 2026** (Amazon's UK & Ireland Country Manager Jonathan Boumphrey: "It takes just seconds to set up... customers selecting their bank from a list that includes more than 99% of UK banks"), with eBay following on 19 February 2026. However, trust for large one-off payments is still maturing and consumer-protection concerns are prominent: **Which? warned consumers on 11 June 2025** — backed by a nationally representative March 2025 survey of 2,500+ adults finding "only one in eight people (13%) were aware they would have no clear purchase protections if they used Pay by Bank to book flights and the airline subsequently stopped trading" — to "think carefully before using it to book events or make substantial purchases." **Martin Lewis (MoneySavingExpert) similarly warned in January 2025**: "be aware there's LITTLE PROTECTION... ultimately it's just a bank transfer, which means you don't get the same refund rights, like chargeback or Section 75, if things go wrong." This is directly material to weddings.

### 2. Regulatory and legal context

**Surcharge ban (Consumer Rights (Payment Surcharges) Regulations 2012, as amended by PSRs 2017 from 13 Jan 2018).** It is illegal to surcharge a consumer for paying by **consumer debit/credit card or "similar" instruments (e.g. PayPal, e-money)**. What remains legal and directly useful to Venviewer: **surcharging commercial/corporate cards** (capped at the cost borne by the merchant); **minimum-spend rules**; charging for other methods; and — most importantly — **offering a discount for paying by bank transfer/Direct Debit** (a discount for a cheaper method is not a banned surcharge). The determinant is the payment method, not the buyer: a sole trader using a personal card is protected; a corporate card, even for a personal purchase, is not. Enforcement is via Trading Standards; banned surcharges are unenforceable and refundable.

**Section 75 Consumer Credit Act 1974.** For credit-card purchases where the item/service costs **more than £100 and up to £30,000**, the card issuer is **jointly and severally liable** with the supplier for breach of contract or misrepresentation — and this applies **even if only the deposit (as little as 1p) was paid on the credit card**, covering the entire purchase price. This is squarely a wedding scenario: a Financial Ombudsman case study on a £5,000 cancelled booking where the customer paid "£1,250 on their credit card and the remaining £3,750 by bank transfer" found the issuer "should give the customer all of the money back with interest." Implication: **clients have a rational incentive to pay deposits by credit card**, the platform/venue should expect it, and taking most of the money by bank transfer does **not** extinguish the exposure — one credit-card pound on the deposit puts the whole contract value in scope.

**Chargeback exposure on deposits for future services.** Under Visa rules (reason code 13.1, merchandise/services not received), the cardholder generally has **120 days from the expected delivery/service date**, capped at **540 days from the transaction date**. For a wedding booked 12–24 months out this is decisive: a deposit taken today can be charged back **up to ~18 months later**, with the 120-day clock running from the wedding date, not the payment date. On venue insolvency or cancellation, acquirers hold the merchant liable; because the merchant may be gone, **acquirers/platforms hold reserves and rolling holdbacks against this future-delivery risk**. Mastercard uses a similar 120-day window that can run from the service date.

**FCA safeguarding / payment-institution status.** If Venviewer sits **in the flow of funds** (receiving client money into an account it controls before passing it to the venue), it likely becomes a regulated payment institution and triggers **safeguarding obligations**. The FCA's strengthened safeguarding regime (PS25/12, published 7 August 2025) states: "The Supplementary Regime, and related amendments to the Approach Document, will come into force on **7 May 2026**," adding daily reconciliations, resolution packs, monthly returns and annual safeguarding audits — with an audit exemption only below £100,000 of relevant funds over 53 weeks (per Norton Rose Fulbright's summary, that threshold "exempts approximately 23% of payments firms holding funds whilst leaving only £3.2 million of customer funds out of over £27 billion total unaudited"). The **commercial-agent exclusion** in the PSRs 2017 does **not** apply where a business acts for both payer and payee and holds funds before settling the payee — exactly the merchant-of-record trap. The clean answer is to **stay out of the flow of funds**: with Stripe Connect (venue as merchant of record) and GoCardless (direct merchant relationship with the venue), the platform never holds client money and avoids the regime entirely.

### 3. Platform structure for a multi-tenant venue SaaS

**Stripe Connect account/charge types and liability.**
- **Direct charges (Standard/Express connected accounts):** the charge lives on the **venue's** account; refunds and chargebacks hit the **venue's** balance first. Negative-balance responsibility can be assigned to **Stripe** (recommended for new platforms) or to the platform. Stripe explicitly recommends direct charges assign negative-balance liability to Stripe.
- **Destination charges / separate charges and transfers:** the charge lives on the **platform's** account and the **platform is ultimately liable for chargebacks and negative balances**; card networks monitor the **platform's** dispute rate. Using `on_behalf_of` sets the venue as merchant of record for the payment.
- Where the platform is liable, Stripe holds a **Connect reserve** on the platform's balance equal to negative connected-account balances, and after **180 days** pulls platform funds to clear a persistently negative venue balance.

**Onboarding friction.** Standard accounts: Stripe runs KYC/KYB; quickest to integrate, least platform control, venue interacts with Stripe. Express: Stripe still handles KYC but the venue lives inside Venviewer's UI; ~10–15 min onboarding, first payout typically ~7 days after first payment, with a 7-day grace period if verification info needs fixing. Custom/own-merchant-account: full underwriting per venue, slowest, most control, most liability/complexity on the platform. (Note: Stripe's legacy Standard/Express/Custom labels are being superseded by the Accounts v2 controller-property model; the liability principles below still hold.)

**Venue holding its own merchant account (platform never MoR):** cleanest liability isolation and direct payout to the venue, but full merchant underwriting per venue is heavy onboarding friction for small venues and more integration work. **Stripe Connect Standard achieves most of the same isolation with far less friction** and is the pragmatic middle path.

**GoCardless for Partners / Embed and TrueLayer:** GoCardless's partner model has **GoCardless establish the direct relationship with each venue** and process on its behalf; the platform can earn a **revenue share** and add app fees (capped at 50% of the payment, and refunds require the venue be on the Pro plan). This keeps the platform out of the flow of funds. TrueLayer offers merchant/marketplace-style integrations but is enterprise/quote-only.

**Merchant-of-record posture (the central risk decision).** If Venviewer becomes MoR or sits in the flow of funds, it inherits: (a) the venue's chargeback/Section 75/future-delivery liability, (b) reserve requirements, (c) FCA safeguarding/PI authorisation, and (d) the catastrophic scenario — **a venue going bust with a large book of future wedding deposits taken through the platform, where cardholders charge back up to 540 days later and the platform, as MoR, is left holding the loss**. The recommendation is unambiguous: **keep each venue as its own merchant of record and keep the platform out of the flow of funds.**

### 4. Economics worked through (Standard pricing; venue absorbs fees due to surcharge ban)

| Payment | Stripe UK card (1.5%+20p) | Stripe intl card (3.25%+20p) | GoCardless Direct Debit (1%+20p, cap £4, +0.3% HVT >£2k) | GoCardless Instant Bank Pay (cap £4, no HVT) | TrueLayer/open-banking (benchmark) |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| £500 deposit | £7.70 | £16.45 | £4.00 | £4.00 | ~£0.50–£2.50 |
| £5,000 instalment | £75.20 | £162.70 | £13.00 | £4.00 | ~£0.50–£5 |
| £12,000 final balance | £180.20 | £390.20 | £34.00 | £4.00* | ~£0.50–£5 |

*Subject to the IBP per-transaction limit; GoCardless materials cite conflicting maxima (a £25,000 figure in one FAQ, a £5,000 figure in the partner checklist), so the £12,000 case must be validated against the live limit for the integration. All figures exclude VAT (GoCardless adds UK VAT on its fees; Stripe fees are pre-VAT).

The gap is stark: on a £12,000 balance, cards cost the venue ~£180 (domestic) to ~£390 (international); bank rails cost £4–£34. Across a typical wedding (deposit + instalment + balance ≈ £17,500), all-card could cost the venue roughly £260 domestic / ~£570 international, versus ~£12 via pay-by-bank. Because surcharging consumer cards is illegal, **every pound of card fee comes straight out of venue margin** unless steered to bank rails via a legal bank-transfer discount.

## Details

The strategic tension is between **cost/settlement** (which favour open-banking pay-by-bank overwhelmingly — instant Faster Payments settlement, ~£4 or less on any ticket size, no chargebacks) and **consumer-protection expectations** (which favour credit cards, because Section 75 gives clients powerful recourse on a wedding booked far in advance, and both Which? and Martin Lewis have publicly told consumers that pay-by-bank lacks it). Direct Debit sits awkwardly for this use case: its cost advantage is real for recurring instalments, but the £2,000 HVT surcharge erodes it at balance sizes, the ~7-working-day first payment is slow, and the unlimited-in-time Direct Debit Guarantee makes it a poor fit for large one-off deposits a client might later reverse. Instant Bank Pay is the better bank rail here because it keeps the £4 cap, has no HVT surcharge, settles same-day, and needs no mandate.

Open banking's Achilles heel for weddings is refunds and protection. Refunds are not native to the rail: TrueLayer implements closed-loop refunds/payouts (instant, but only back to the original payer, capped at the original amount) and GoCardless processes IBP/pay-by-bank refunds only when the merchant is on the appropriate plan and requests them (and not at all for IBP with direct settlement). The client portal must therefore model refunds differently per rail. And because there is no Section 75/chargeback on pay-by-bank, a deposit-protection-conscious client may insist on a card — which the venue should accommodate on the deposit specifically, where the absolute fee is small and the protection expectation is highest.

## Recommendations

**Staged, concrete next steps:**

1. **P1 build — hybrid rails with per-milestone defaults.**
   - **Booking deposit (£500–£2,000):** present **card (Stripe) and pay-by-bank side by side**, defaulting to pay-by-bank but clearly offering card "for Section 75 protection." The absolute card fee here is small (~£8–£30) and matching client protection expectations reduces friction at the point of commitment. Accept that this is also the highest future-delivery chargeback-exposure payment; keep the deposit modest to cap that exposure.
   - **50%-at-6-months instalment and final balance (£5k–£15k):** default to **open-banking pay-by-bank (GoCardless Instant Bank Pay first, given the £4 cap and no HVT; TrueLayer as the higher-conversion network alternative)**, with card available only as a fallback. This is where the fee savings are decisive (£4 vs £75–£180+).
   - **Offer a legal bank-transfer/pay-by-bank discount** (e.g. 1–1.5%) to nudge clients off cards on the large payments — permitted because it is a discount for a cheaper method, not a banned surcharge.

2. **Platform structure — Stripe Connect Standard (venue = merchant of record), negative-balance liability assigned to Stripe**, plus **GoCardless for Partners/Embed** for bank rails with GoCardless holding the direct venue relationship. Do **not** become merchant of record and do **not** take client funds into a platform-controlled account. This avoids FCA PI authorisation/safeguarding (PS25/12, live 7 May 2026) and isolates venue-insolvency risk from the platform.

3. **Product surfaces to build:** a per-milestone payment-method selector with protection/settlement messaging; refund handling that branches by rail (card refund = fee not returned, 5–10 days; pay-by-bank = provider-specific closed-loop refund; Direct Debit = indemnity-claim awareness); bank-transfer-discount mechanics; and clear advance-notice/cancellation flows if Direct Debit is used for instalments (to minimise indemnity claims and "double-dip" refund fraud).

**Benchmarks/thresholds that would change the recommendation:**
- If a venue's international-client share is high, card fees climb to 3.25%+2% and the case for steering to pay-by-bank strengthens further.
- If pay-by-bank conversion proves poor in live A/B tests (unsupported bank, trust hesitancy on £10k+ payments), keep card as a prominent fallback and revisit the defaults — cart-abandonment when a preferred method is unavailable is real (roughly one in five consumers).
- If Venviewer's economics ever push it toward holding funds (escrow, split disbursement), that triggers FCA safeguarding and a full re-architecture — treat crossing into the flow of funds as a hard gate requiring legal review.
- Validate the live GoCardless IBP per-transaction ceiling before relying on it for £12k+ balances.

## Caveats
- **TrueLayer and most open-banking providers are quote-only**; the 0.1%–1.0% / 20–50p benchmark is from third-party aggregators (Noda publishes "from 0.1%"; Volt's implied rate is an outlier), not primary rate cards. Obtain written quotes before final modelling.
- **GoCardless IBP transaction-limit sources conflict** (£25,000 vs £5,000); confirm for the specific integration path.
- Stripe's legacy Standard/Express/Custom account-type terminology is being replaced by the Accounts v2 controller-property model; the liability principles hold but the API surface is changing.
- The FCA safeguarding end-state ("Post-Repeal" statutory-trust regime) is not yet in force and its timing is uncertain; the **7 May 2026 Supplementary Regime is the operative deadline**.
- Chargeback/Section 75 outcomes are fact-specific and adjudicated case by case; the figures here (120/540-day windows, £100–£30,000 thresholds) are the rules, not guarantees of outcome.
- Fees quoted exclude VAT (GoCardless adds UK VAT on its fees; Stripe fees are pre-VAT). This brief is decision-support, not legal advice — confirm the surcharge-ban and safeguarding positions with UK payments counsel before launch.
