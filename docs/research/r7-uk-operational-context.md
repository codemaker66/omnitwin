# R7 — UK Venue Operational Context for Event-Planning Software (Awareness Only, Not Legal Advice)

**Important framing:** This report is general awareness / typical-practice information to inform product modelling of a "Day Sheet" planning tool. It is **not legal advice**. Licence conditions are premises-specific, set by individual licensing boards/authorities, and change over time. Operators must consult their own premises licence, their licensing board/local authority, and professional advisers before relying on any end-time or procedure.

## TL;DR

- **Model the curfew line and door/alarm sections as operator-entered planning fields, never as compliance claims.** In Scotland there is no national closing time: a Licensing Board exists for each local authority area (or licensing division) — 35 boards across the 32 authorities — and each sets its own terminal hours through its statement of licensing policy (currently on 2023–2028 cycles) and the individual premises' operating plan, so any end-time is specific to that licence and can change.
- **The requester used the England-and-Wales term "TEN," but Scotland has no TENs.** Temporary Event Notices exist only under the Licensing Act 2003 (England & Wales). Scotland uses "occasional licences" and "extended hours applications" under the Licensing (Scotland) Act 2005. The software should not conflate the two systems.
- **Noise curfews, door/window conditions, alarm zoning, keyholder response and fire-alarm isolation are all premises-, building- and board-specific.** They flow from separate legal regimes (licensing, planning, statutory nuisance, fire safety, police alarm policy, SIA licensing) that the software cannot assert as satisfied. Treat every such field as a prompt for the operator to record their own arrangements.

## Key Findings

1. **Scottish licensed hours are set per-premises, not nationally.** Under the Licensing (Scotland) Act 2005, licensed hours are whatever is specified in the operating plan contained in the premises licence. There is a statutory presumption against 24-hour opening (s.64). Boards publish policy terminal hours; typical patterns are midnight/1am for pubs and 3am (occasionally 4am) for nightclubs in city centres.
2. **Glasgow and Edinburgh illustrate the variability.** Glasgow made a 4am terminal hour for city-centre nightclubs permanent following a pilot, and ran a 1am pilot for city-centre pubs/bars (default 12 midnight). Edinburgh's policy sets a general on-sales terminal of 1am, 3am for entertainment/restaurant premises, 6am for casinos, with a 2-hour extension during the Festival and festive periods where the operating plan opts in.
3. **TENs (England & Wales) vs occasional licences (Scotland) are different mechanisms** with different caps, applicants and notice periods.
4. **Noise controls are layered:** licence conditions, planning conditions, and statutory nuisance under the Environmental Protection Act 1990 all operate separately. Typical conditions include inaudibility/"no audible nuisance" at nearest residential premises, doors/windows closed, and music wind-down before close.
5. **Access/security choreography is heavily building-specific:** key registers, master/suited keys, part-set alarm zones, URN/keyholder police response, freight-lift/loading-bay booking, listed-building constraints, fire-alarm isolation for haze/pyro, and SIA door supervision.

## Details

### 1. Scottish premises licensing and event end-times

**The operating plan drives everything.** Under the Licensing (Scotland) Act 2005 an applicant must submit an operating plan (form prescribed by the Premises Licence (Scotland) Regulations 2007) stating, for each day, the opening time and the "terminal hour" for on-sales and off-sales, plus which additional activities (recorded music, live performance, dance, etc.) occur within and outwith "core hours." "Licensed hours" are defined (s.62) as the periods specified in the operating plan. Compliance with the operating plan is a condition of the licence (s.27(1) and schedule 3). Off-sales are fixed nationally at 10am–10pm (s.65(3)); there is no discretion to vary these.

**No national closing time; boards set policy.** A Licensing Board exists for each local authority area, or for each division where an area is divided into licensing divisions (2005 Act s.5) — 35 boards across the 32 authorities — each with its own statement of licensing policy setting typical terminal hours, published in five-year cycles (current statements cover 2023–2028). There is a strong statutory presumption against 24-hour opening under s.64, which boards may disapply only in "exceptional circumstances" (Scottish Government guidance suggests one-off festivals may qualify). Premises opening after 1am attract additional mandatory conditions. Scottish Government guidance notes that applications for licensed hours of more than 14 hours require further consideration.

**Typical terminal-hour patterns.** Pubs commonly close around midnight or 1am; nightclubs and casinos in major city centres can trade to 3am or 4am on Friday/Saturday nights.

- **Glasgow:** the general city-centre terminal hour for pubs/bars has been 12 midnight; a 4am terminal hour for city-centre nightclubs was trialled and then made permanent, awarded to venues demonstrating higher operating standards ("gold status"). A separate 12-month pilot allowed certain city-centre on-sales premises to trade to 1am instead of midnight, requiring a Major Variation application by 31 January 2024. Glasgow's night-time economy has been valued by the Moffat Centre (Glasgow Caledonian University) at around £2.16 billion per annum (activity between 6pm and 6am), supporting some 16,600 full-time jobs.
- **Edinburgh (per the City of Edinburgh Licensing Board Statement of Licensing Policy 2023):** opening 9am (11am Sundays); off-sales 10am–10pm; **terminal hours — off-sales 10pm; licensed premises generally 1am; premises offering restaurant facilities or entertainment (and members' clubs) 3am; adult entertainment 1am; casinos 6am.** The Board usually regards up to 14 trading hours as reasonable (16 considered case by case). During the Edinburgh Festival/Fringe and the Christmas–New Year period a **2-hour extension** to the usual terminal hour is acceptable where the operating plan states an intention to use it (so a 3am entertainment venue could reach 5am).

**Extended hours applications and "occasional extensions."** Boards can extend licensed hours on application by a premises licence holder for a special event/occasion (s.67); the chief constable and Licensing Standards Officer are notified and may object/comment. Extended-hours periods are capped at a maximum of one month. Boards also publish festive policies granting blanket extensions (e.g., Hogmanay), which apply automatically only where the operating plan references the board's festive policy.

**"Last entry" and "drinking-up time" conventions.** During late-night extensions, some boards enforce an entry curfew (a last-entry time, often midnight/1am) after which no new or returning customers are admitted even though the venue trades later. After last orders, a drinking-up time applies — the 2005 Act explanatory notes describe a 15- or 30-minute drinking-up period as an exception to the offence of consuming alcohol outwith licensed hours (commonly 15 minutes, or 30 minutes with a meal). Note: the 15/30-minute figure derives from the Act/regulations, not from Edinburgh's board policy, which instead speaks of "wind down periods" and expects CCTV to run for at least 30 minutes after the terminal hour. British Summer Time clock changes are handled by counting hours after midnight (s.66), so venues neither lose nor gain trading time.

**Scotland vs England & Wales.** England & Wales operate under the Licensing Act 2003 (premises licences, designated premises supervisors, four licensing objectives incl. prevention of public nuisance). Scotland's 2005 Act uses licensing boards, operating plans, core/terminal hours, Licensing Standards Officers, a fifth objective (protecting and improving public health), and different temporary-authorisation mechanisms. The requester asked about Scotland but used the E&W term "TEN"; the two systems must not be conflated.

### 2. Temporary Event Notices (E&W) vs occasional licences (Scotland)

**Temporary Event Notices — Licensing Act 2003, England & Wales only.** A TEN is a light-touch notification allowing licensable activities (sale of alcohol, regulated entertainment, late-night refreshment) at a temporary event. Key limits:
- **Attendance cap: no more than 499 people at any one time**, including staff, performers and stewards. 500+ requires a premises licence.
- **Duration:** an event may last up to 168 hours (7 days).
- **Per-premises limits:** up to 15 TENs per premises per calendar year, aggregate maximum 21 days per year; events crossing midnight count as two days; minimum 24 hours between separate TENs at the same premises.
- **Per-user limits:** personal licence holders may give up to 50 TENs a year (of which up to 10 late); non-personal-licence holders up to 5 (of which up to 2 late).
- **Notice periods:** a **standard TEN** requires at least 10 clear working days' notice; a **late TEN** may be given 5–9 working days before the event.
- **Objections:** the police and the environmental health authority may object within 3 working days on any of the four licensing objectives. For a late TEN, a single objection ends the event.

**Scotland has NO TENs.** The equivalents under the Licensing (Scotland) Act 2005 are:
- **Occasional licences (s.56):** authorise the sale of alcohol on unlicensed premises for a period of not more than 14 days. Applicants may be a premises licence holder, a personal licence holder, or a representative of a voluntary organisation (voluntary organisations only for events connected with their activities — mandatory condition 5, schedule 4). Fee historically £10. Police Scotland may recommend refusal and the LSO may report within 21 days; any person may object within 7 days of publication on the Tell Me Scotland portal. **Voluntary-organisation caps (s.56(6)):** no more than 4 occasional licences of 4+ days and no more than 12 of under 4 days in any 12 months, subject to a combined maximum of 56 days per year. Premises/personal licence holders currently face no statutory numerical cap (the Scottish Government has consulted on introducing limits and raising the fee). Boards commonly require applications several weeks ahead (e.g., Glasgow 7 weeks; some boards 35 days) and may require SIA-licensed stewards for certain events.
- **Extended hours applications (s.67):** extend the hours of an existing premises licence for a special occasion (see section 1).

### 3. Noise and curfew conditions on historic city venues

**Three separate regimes.** Noise is controlled through (a) licence conditions set by the licensing board, (b) planning conditions set by the planning authority, and (c) statutory nuisance under the Environmental Protection Act 1990. Scottish Government guidance stresses that planning, building control and licensing are separate processes considered on their own merits; Edinburgh's policy explicitly states the Board's licensing functions are discharged separately from the Council's planning functions.

**Typical licence noise conditions.** Common patterns at licensed premises include: amplified music/vocals to be inaudible (or, in Edinburgh's 2023 formulation, "not an audible nuisance") in the nearest noise-sensitive/residential premises; doors and windows kept closed during regulated entertainment; installation of acoustic lobbies, soundproofing, air conditioning/mechanical ventilation and sound-limiting devices; the premises manager (not the DJ) to control sound levels; and volume reduced substantially after midnight. Edinburgh's occasional-licence "pool conditions" include alternatives such as "All amplified music and vocals shall be controlled as to be inaudible in the nearest noise sensitive premises after 23.00 hours" and a stricter all-hours inaudibility version. Music-off-before-close conventions (typically 30–60 minutes before the terminal hour, with gradual lighting increase) form part of the "wind-down" expectation.

**Outdoor and festival venues.** For outdoor areas Edinburgh treats 10pm as the appropriate terminal hour (marquees/tents count as "outdoor space"), with later hours considered case by case and not automatically covered by the 2-hour festival extension. Edinburgh Fringe temporary venues generally operate on occasional licences plus a public entertainment licence, and are subject to the board's pool conditions on noise, dispersal and children's access. Open-air concert guidance (e.g., council guidance building on the Noise Council's Pop Code and the Purple Guide) typically involves a noise management plan, a noise consultant, propagation testing, real-time perimeter dB monitoring, directional speaker/cardioid-subwoofer design, hard curfews, and music-level guideline limits at the nearest noise-sensitive premises. The Noise Council's *Code of Practice on Environmental Noise Control at Concerts* (1995) recommends a Music Noise Level limit of 65 dB(A) LAeq at the nearest noise-sensitive premises for urban/rural venues and 75 dB(A) for urban stadia/arenas; higher figures may be negotiated for events on a limited number of days per year. Load-out and deliveries in residential areas are commonly restricted to daytime hours to avoid night-time nuisance.

**Statutory nuisance (Environmental Protection Act 1990).** A local authority must investigate complaints and, if satisfied a statutory nuisance exists (noise "prejudicial to health or a nuisance"), must serve an abatement notice (s.80). Breach without reasonable excuse is an offence; on industrial, trade or business premises the maximum fine on summary conviction is £40,000 (s.80(6); some older council guidance still cites the pre-amendment £20,000 cap). A key defence for business premises is that the **"best practicable means"** were used to prevent/counteract the nuisance. Appeals go to the magistrates'/sheriff court within 21 days. Sections 79–82 extend to Scotland (from 1996).

**Agent of change.** This principle places responsibility for mitigating noise impacts on the party introducing the change (e.g., a developer building housing near an existing venue must fund soundproofing). It is embedded in England's National Planning Policy Framework; in Scotland the Chief Planner's February 2018 letter confirmed the intention to include it in national planning policy. Crucially, the Scottish Chief Planner's letter states this "does not affect the operation of noise nuisance controls under the Environmental Protection Act, nor any consideration of licensing issues" — so agent of change does not immunise a venue from statutory nuisance or licence review.

### 4. Access/security choreography at multi-tenant historic buildings

**Key handover and registers.** Standard practice in multi-tenant/heritage buildings uses documented key control: a key register recording key type, keyholder name, date issued and return/termination date; restricted/patented keyways so keys cannot be freely copied; and master-key/suited-key hierarchies giving building managers access to common areas while tenants hold keys only to their space. End-of-night choreography typically involves a sweep, setting the alarm, and returning/signing in keys.

**Alarm zoning and part-setting.** Shared buildings commonly divide intruder-alarm coverage into zones so one tenant's area can be set/part-set while others remain occupied; event access must be coordinated so the correct zones are unset for the event footprint and reset at lock-up.

**Police response, URNs and keyholders (NPCC Security Systems Policy).** Monitored intruder alarms that meet standards and are installed by registered companies are "Type A" systems issued a Unique Reference Number (URN) and monitored via an Alarm Receiving Centre (ARC). Police response is graded; after three false calls in a rolling 12-month period, response is withdrawn to "Level 3 — keyholder response only." The ARC must hold details of (typically two) keyholders able to attend within 20 minutes; persistent keyholder non-attendance can lead to withdrawal of police response. Non-compliant "Type B" systems get no guaranteed police response. The policy is published via Secured by Design / policesecuritysystems.com. Event planners at shared buildings must ensure keyholder/ARC details and access lists are current for the event.

**Building management and facilities coordination.** Practical event access typically requires booking freight lifts and loading-bay slots, and being added to out-of-hours access authorisation lists maintained by building management/FM. These are operational arrangements agreed with the landlord/FM, not licence matters.

**Listed-building and conservation-area constraints.** In Scotland, listed building consent (administered by the planning authority under the Planning (Listed Buildings and Conservation Areas) (Scotland) Act 1997, with Historic Environment Scotland consulted on certain cases) is required for interior or exterior works affecting a building's special character; unauthorised works are a criminal offence. This constrains physical security measures (external shutters, roller blinds, alarm boxes, cabling) and signage on historic venues; advertisement consent may also be required. Per Historic Environment Scotland, "In March 2026, Scotland had more than 46,500 listed building records" and "Scotland currently has more than 600 conservation areas."

**Fire safety and detector isolation for haze/pyrotechnics (Fire (Scotland) Act 2005).** In Scotland non-domestic fire safety is governed by the Fire (Scotland) Act 2005 and the Fire Safety (Scotland) Regulations 2006, which place duties on the duty-holder (employer/premises manager) including a fire risk assessment and maintained detection/warning systems. Haze, smoke and pyrotechnic effects can trigger smoke detectors, so events often require temporary, controlled isolation/zoning of specific detectors — which must be managed within the fire risk assessment and reinstated afterwards, with alternative measures (e.g., fire watch) in place. Knowingly giving a false fire alarm is an offence (s.85). In multi-tenant buildings, isolating a detector zone affects other occupants and must be coordinated with building management and the alarm/monitoring provider.

**SIA-licensed door supervision.** Under the Private Security Industry Act 2001, staff performing door-supervision functions (controlling entry, checking IDs, managing queues, ejecting) at licensed premises must hold an SIA Door Supervisor licence — this applies whether staff are contracted or in-house, and applies in Scotland via the Licensing (Scotland) Act 2005 (Consequential Provisions) Order 2009. At mixed event sites only the "licensable area" (e.g., a bar or beer tent) may require door supervisors; the licensable area is usually shown on the plan accompanying the licence application. A TEN or occasional licence can temporarily make a space "licensed premises" for these purposes.

### 5. Synthesis for product modelling

Every end-time, curfew, door/window rule and alarm/keyholder procedure examined above is **premises-, building- and board/authority-specific**, and is the product of multiple overlapping legal regimes that change over time. Consequently, in the Day Sheet product:

- The **curfew line** should be an **operator-entered field** (the operator records their own licensed terminal hour, any last-entry time, music-off time and drinking-up window, based on their premises licence and operating plan). The software should not assert or imply that a given end-time is legally compliant, permitted, or "the" closing time — because it depends on that specific licence and can be varied or reviewed.
- The **door/alarm sections** (key handover, alarm set/unset zones, keyholder/ARC details, access authorisation, fire-detector isolation, door supervision) should likewise be **operator-entered planning data** reflecting the venue's own building arrangements and contracts — never automatically asserted legal facts or compliance guarantees.
- The product should present all such fields as **planning prompts**, ideally with a persistent disclaimer that the information is operational planning data entered by the operator, is not a compliance determination, and that operators must consult their own premises licence, licensing board/local authority, building management, and professional advisers.

## Recommendations

1. **Model curfew and door/alarm data as free/structured operator input, not system-asserted facts.** Label these fields explicitly as "operator-entered planning data." Trigger to change: none — this should be a fixed design principle.
2. **Separate the Scotland and England & Wales vocabularies in the UI.** Do not surface "TEN" for Scottish venues; use "occasional licence" / "extended hours application." Where the tool serves both jurisdictions, key the terminology to the venue's location.
3. **Add non-blocking helper text, not validation.** E.g., next to the curfew line, note that terminal hours are set per premises by the licensing board and that music-off/last-entry/drinking-up times may be earlier — without validating or "approving" any value the operator enters.
4. **Include a standing disclaimer** on any screen modelling curfew or security procedures: general planning support only, not legal/compliance advice; consult the premises licence, board/authority and advisers.
5. **Provide structured fields for building-specific security items** (alarm zones, keyholder/ARC contacts, freight-lift/loading-bay bookings, fire-detector isolation sign-off, SIA cover) so operators capture their own arrangements — but avoid any "compliant/OK" status indicator that could imply legal assurance. Threshold to revisit: if a future feature attempts automated compliance checking, it should be gated behind explicit professional/legal review and jurisdiction-specific data.

## Caveats

- **This is general awareness information, not legal advice.** All figures are typical patterns/ranges, not definitive rules.
- **Everything is premises- and board/authority-specific and subject to change.** Terminal hours, noise conditions, festive/festival extensions and occasional-licence caps differ between boards and are periodically revised; the Scottish Government has consulted on new occasional-licence limits and fees.
- **Some sources are secondary or commercial** (e.g., aggregator and vendor pages on alarm/access-control practice, and a commercial "24 Hour Alcohol" summary of Scottish hours). These were used for typical-practice context; primary legal points are drawn from legislation.gov.uk, gov.scot, and board policy documents. Where a specific condition's current wording matters (e.g., Edinburgh's shift from "inaudible" to "no audible nuisance"), operators should check the current published policy.
- **The Glasgow night-time economy figure** (~£2.16 billion / 16,600 FTE jobs) is from a Moffat Centre (GCU) study; a widely circulated "£2.6 billion" figure appears to misstate it.
- **Agent of change is a planning principle, not a shield** against statutory nuisance or licence review, and in Scotland its statutory footing has developed through planning policy rather than a standalone Act provision.
- **The Day Sheet must not represent or imply legal compliance** of any end-time or procedure; that determination rests with the operator and their advisers.
